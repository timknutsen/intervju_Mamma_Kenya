# British Colonisation of Kenya

Research notes compiled for the historical context layer of the Kenya Story project.

## Pre-colonial Kenya

Kenya was home to more than forty distinct peoples before European contact. The Kikuyu farmed the fertile highlands around Mount Kenya. The Maasai moved with their cattle across the Rift Valley. The Luo fished on Lake Victoria. The Kamba traded between coast and interior. Along the coast, Swahili city-states had traded with Arabia, Persia, and India for centuries.

The Berlin Conference of 1884-85 carved Africa into European spheres of influence. Britain and Germany agreed on East African divisions in 1886, refined in the Heligoland-Zanzibar Treaty of 1890.

In the 1890s, rinderpest killed vast herds of cattle, followed by smallpox and famine. The Kikuyu population was severely reduced. British administrators looked at depopulated land and called it "empty."

## Imperial British East Africa Company (IBEA)

- 1888: IBEA granted Royal Charter. Key figure: Sir William Mackinnon (Scottish shipping magnate).
- Purpose: administer British sphere of influence commercially without government expense.
- The company was chronically underfunded and unable to generate profits.
- 1895: IBEA wound up; British government took over directly.

## East Africa Protectorate to Kenya Colony

- 1 July 1895: East Africa Protectorate declared, under Foreign Office.
- 1902: Administration transferred to Colonial Office. Crown Lands Ordinance declared "waste and unoccupied" land as Crown land.
- 1905: Capital moved from Mombasa to Nairobi.
- 1915: Crown Lands Ordinance strengthened. All land became Crown property. Leases to Europeans up to 999 years. African rights reduced to tenancy.
- 11 June 1920: Formally renamed Colony and Protectorate of Kenya.

## The Uganda Railway ("Lunatic Express")

- Authorized 1895. Construction began 1896 from Mombasa.
- Purpose: secure Uganda and the Nile headwaters; suppress slave trade.
- ~32,000 indentured labourers from British India. Hundreds died from disease, accidents, and the Tsavo man-eaters (1898).
- Chief Engineer: George Whitehouse.
- Reached Nairobi 1899. Reached Kisumu (Lake Victoria) December 1901.
- Cost: ~5.5 million GBP.
- The railway was the defining factor in opening the highlands to settlement. The government encouraged European farming along the corridor to generate freight revenue.

## White Highlands

- ~16,700 square miles of fertile highland territory reserved exclusively for European settlement.
- Key areas: Nairobi, Thika, Nyeri, Nanyuki, Nakuru, Eldoret, Rift Valley.
- Kikuyu land around Kiambu, Thika, and Murang'a was systematically alienated.
- 1904 and 1911 Maasai Agreements moved Maasai out of the Rift Valley.
- 1939 Kenya (Highlands) Order in Council formally reserved the White Highlands for Europeans.
- Displaced Africans confined to overcrowded "Native Reserves" or became squatter labourers on European farms.
- Kipande system (from 1920): all adult African men required to carry registration passes.

## Early settlers

- **Lord Delamere** (Hugh Cholmondeley, 3rd Baron, 1870-1931): arrived 1897, acquired 100,000+ acres in Rift Valley. Political leader of settler community.
- **Happy Valley set**: wealthy aristocrats in Wanjohi Valley, 1920s-1940s. Notorious lifestyle. Not representative of most settlers.
- **Karen Blixen** (Isak Dinesen): Danish, coffee farm near Nairobi 1914-1931. *Out of Africa* (1937).
- **Elspeth Huxley**: grew up on coffee farm near Thika. *The Flame Trees of Thika* (1959).
- **Norwegian settlers**: small but notable community. The Koren family and Kopperud brothers documented in *Nordmenn i det koloniale Kenya*.

## Why Britain wanted settlers

- **Strategic**: Suez Canal route to India; control of Nile headwaters via Uganda; counter Germany.
- **Economic**: make the railway pay through freight from cash crops (coffee, tea, sisal).
- **Political**: "constructive imperialism" (Joseph Chamberlain). Commissioner Charles Eliot (1900-1904) actively recruited settlers, calling Kenya "a white man's country."
