# Coffee in Colonial Kenya

Research notes compiled for the historical context layer of the Kenya Story project.

## Origins

- Coffee introduced to Kenya in 1893 by French Holy Ghost Fathers (Catholic missionaries) from Reunion Island to St. Austin's Mission near Nairobi.
- Commercial planting began ~1900-1905 after the railway reached Nairobi.
- Original variety: French Mission Bourbon.

## Key varieties

- **French Mission Bourbon**: original variety from Reunion. Good quality but susceptible to coffee berry disease.
- **SL28 and SL34**: developed in the 1930s at Scott Agricultural Laboratories, Nairobi. SL28 selected from drought-resistant Tanganyika stock; SL34 from French Mission. Both famous for bright acidity and complex fruit notes. Dominant commercial varieties by the 1950s.
- **K7**: Scott Labs selection, more disease-resistant but lower quality.

In the 1950s at Rubislaw, the coffee was almost certainly SL28 or SL34.

## The Thika district

- Altitude ~1,500m (4,900 ft) on eastern edge of Central Highlands, ~40 km NE of Nairobi.
- Deep volcanic red soils (nitisols/laterites) from ancient volcanic activity. Among the best coffee soils in the world.
- Bimodal rainfall: "long rains" March-May, "short rains" October-December. ~900-1,100 mm annually.
- Temperatures 15-26 C year-round. No frost. Ideal for slow cherry maturation.
- One of the earliest European farming areas, settled from ~1900.
- Made famous by Elspeth Huxley's *The Flame Trees of Thika* (1959).

## How the plantation worked

### Annual cycle
- Trees take 3-5 years to reach full production.
- Flowering after rain (white blossoms), cherries ripen in ~9 months.
- Main harvest June-July; secondary "fly crop" November-December.
- Harvesting by hand, selectively picking only ripe red cherries.

### Processing (washed/wet method)
1. **Pulping**: machines squeeze off outer red skin and pulp.
2. **Fermentation**: beans in water tanks 24-72 hours to dissolve mucilage.
3. **Washing**: in channels with running water.
4. **Drying**: spread on raised wire-mesh tables ("African beds") in the sun. Workers turned beans regularly. This matches the family memory of "beans spread on long tables" and "warm white beans in her hands."
5. **Hulling**: parchment skin removed at dry mill, revealing green bean for export.

The "coffee plant" in the family story was the on-site wet processing facility.

### Labour
- Workers predominantly Kikuyu from surrounding area.
- **Resident labourers ("squatters")**: lived on the farm in a village, given small plots in exchange for working days. Formalized under Resident Labourers Ordinance 1937.
- **Seasonal pickers**: women and children did much of the picking.
- **Skilled workers**: machine operators, headmen, office assistants, domestic staff.
- Wages very low by European standards.

## Economics

- Kenya's most valuable export crop. 30-40% of total export earnings.
- Korean War boom (1950-51) sent prices to record highs.
- All coffee sold through **Coffee Board of Kenya** (est. 1933) at weekly **Nairobi Coffee Auction**.
- Grading strict: Kenya AA commanded premium prices, especially in Germany and Scandinavia.
- Jakob Prebensen managed Rubislaw but did not own it. The Sutcliffe family owned the plantation.

## The ban on African coffee

- Africans largely prohibited from growing coffee until mid-1950s.
- Official reason: disease control. Real reason: settlers feared competition and loss of cheap labour.
- **Swynnerton Plan (1954)** recommended allowing African smallholders to grow coffee, partly to create loyal middle class during Emergency.
- After independence, African-grown coffee rapidly overtook estate production.

## Rubislaw estate

- Name is Scottish (Rubislaw quarry in Aberdeen). Consistent with British naming conventions.
- Manager-run estate owned by the Sutcliffe family.
- Manager (Jakob) lived on the estate, oversaw operations and accounts.
