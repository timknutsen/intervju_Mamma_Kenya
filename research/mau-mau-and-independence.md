# Mau Mau and Kenya's Path to Independence

Research notes compiled for the historical context layer of the Kenya Story project.

## Causes

### Land grievances (core driver)
- White Highlands reserved exclusively for Europeans. ~30,000 settlers controlled ~12,000 sq mi of best land.
- Over 1 million Kikuyu crowded into shrinking reserves.
- Carter Land Commission (1932-34) investigated African claims but largely upheld European holdings.
- Kikuyu system of land tenure (githaka, clan-based) not recognized by British.

### Labour exploitation
- Kipande system (from 1920): metal pass worn around the neck, documenting employment. Criminal offense not to carry one.
- Squatter rights progressively curtailed from 1930s.
- Drastically lower wages for Africans.

### Political frustration
- Legislative Council dominated by European settlers. No African representation until 1944 (Eliud Mathu, appointed).
- Kenya African Union (KAU, formed 1944) pursued peaceful reform but was blocked.
- Jomo Kenyatta became president of KAU in 1947.
- Returning WWII veterans found no land rights and continued second-class status.

## The Emergency (1952-1960)

### Key events
- **7 October 1952**: Assassination of Senior Chief Waruhiu wa Kungu.
- **20 October 1952**: Governor Sir Evelyn Baring declared State of Emergency.
- **Operation Jock Scott (20-21 Oct 1952)**: Mass arrest of Kenyatta and 182+ others.
- **Lari Massacre (26 March 1953)**: Mau Mau attacked loyalist village, ~97 killed. British reprisals killed 150+.
- **Naivasha Raid (same night)**: Mau Mau attacked police station, seized weapons.
- **Operation Anvil (24 April 1954)**: Screened entire African population of Nairobi. ~30,000 Kikuyu detained.
- **Dedan Kimathi captured**: 21 October 1956. Executed 18 February 1957.
- **Emergency ended**: 12 January 1960.

### British forces
- Regular army, RAF bombers (Lincolns, Harvards), Kenya Police, Kenya Police Reserve.
- Kikuyu Home Guard: ~25,000 loyalist Africans at peak. Crucial role in counter-insurgency.
- The war was as much a Kikuyu civil conflict as anti-colonial uprising.

### Detention camps ("the Pipeline")
- Estimated 80,000-160,000 detainees at peak (figures contested).
- "Pipeline" system from hard-core to open camps.
- Systematic torture, forced labour, starvation, sexual violence (documented by Caroline Elkins).
- 2013: British government formally acknowledged abuse. Settlement of ~19.9 million GBP for 5,228 elderly Kenyans.

### Casualties (contested)
- European civilians killed: 32.
- Loyalist African civilians killed: ~1,800.
- British security forces killed: ~200.
- Official British figures: 11,503 Mau Mau killed, 1,090 hanged.
- Modern scholarship suggests total African deaths significantly higher.

## Impact on settler life in Thika

- Thika surrounded by Kikuyu reserves, focal point of Mau Mau activity.
- Farms enclosed with barbed wire. Inner compound ("boma").
- Guard dogs, night watchmen (usually non-Kikuyu: Maasai or Kamba).
- European men carried firearms at all times. Women trained in firearms.
- Settler homes had reinforced safe rooms. Children taught emergency drills.
- Kenya Police Reserve: many settler men served alongside running their farms.
- Ruck family murders (January 1953) sent shockwaves through European community.

## Key figures

### African leaders
- **Jomo Kenyatta** (~1897-1978): KAU president. Arrested Oct 1952, Kapenguria trial, convicted April 1953. Released 1961. PM at independence, President 1964.
- **Dedan Kimathi** (1920-1957): military leader of forest fighters. Now national hero.
- **Kapenguria Six**: Kenyatta, Bildad Kaggia, Fred Kubai, Paul Ngei, Kung'u Karumba, Achieng' Oneko.

### British governors
- **Sir Philip Mitchell** (1944-52): largely ignored growing crisis.
- **Sir Evelyn Baring** (1952-59): declared Emergency, oversaw counter-insurgency.
- **Sir Patrick Renison** (1959-62): called Kenyatta "leader to darkness and death."

## Lancaster House conferences

- **January-February 1960**: Colonial Secretary Iain Macleod established African majority rule. Watershed moment.
- **February-April 1962**: Framework for self-government. KANU vs KADU debate.
- **September-October 1963**: Finalized independence constitution. Set 12 December 1963.

### Political milestones
- February 1961: First broad-franchise election. KANU won but refused government without Kenyatta.
- August 1961: Kenyatta released.
- May-June 1963: Pre-independence elections. KANU won. Kenyatta PM on 1 June 1963.

## Uhuru

- **12 December 1963**: Independence. Midnight ceremony in Nairobi.
- **12 December 1964**: Republic. Kenyatta as President.
- Policy of reconciliation. "Willing Buyer, Willing Seller" land transfer programme.

## The settler exodus

- Peak European population: ~60,000-80,000 (early 1950s).
- 1962 census: ~55,000 Europeans.
- Major departures 1960-1964, triggered by first Lancaster House conference.
- Destinations: UK, South Africa, Rhodesia, Australia. Some Scandinavians returned home.
- The Prebensen family left in 1962, sailing from Mombasa via Cape Town to Norway.
