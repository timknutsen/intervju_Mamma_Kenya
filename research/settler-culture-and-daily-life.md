# Settler Culture and Daily Life in Colonial Kenya

Research notes compiled for the historical context layer of the Kenya Story project.

## The club system

- Central social institution. Nearly every town had one.
- Typical facilities: bar, veranda, dining room, tennis courts, swimming pool, billiard room.
- **Muthaiga Club** (Nairobi, founded 1913): most prestigious, unofficial social HQ of the colony.
- District clubs in Thika, Nyeri, Nanyuki, Nakuru, Kitale, Eldoret.
- Clubs enforced racial segregation. Europeans only.
- Horse racing at Ngong Racecourse was a major social event.
- Polo, cricket, rugby, golf also significant.

## Happy Valley

- Wealthy aristocratic settlers in Wanjohi Valley (Aberdare area), 1920s-1940s.
- Notorious for drug use, heavy drinking, adultery.
- "Are you married or do you live in Kenya?"
- Key figures: Josslyn Hay (Lord Erroll, murdered 1941), Lady Idina Sackville, Alice de Janze.
- **Not representative.** A few dozen families at most. Most settlers were working farmers.
- James Fox, *White Mischief* (1982); film 1987.

## Scandinavian settlers

- Small but notable community within predominantly British settler population.
- Norwegians drawn in interwar and postwar periods, often through chain migration.
- The Koren family and Kopperud brothers documented in *Nordmenn i det koloniale Kenya* (held at Nasjonalbiblioteket).
- Settled in highland farming areas: Nairobi, Thika, central highlands.
- Participated in broader European social world while maintaining Norwegian traditions privately.
- Karen Blixen (Danish) is the most famous Scandinavian settler.
- Some Lutheran congregations and Scandinavian cultural associations.

## Household structure

### Typical staff
- **Cook (mpishi)**: senior domestic employee, almost always male. European-style meals.
- **Kitchen boy (kitchen toto)**: assistant. Despite name, adult men.
- **Aya (ayah)**: female nursemaid. Portuguese-via-Swahili term. Closest adult figure to children.
- **House servant / houseboy**: cleaning, serving, laundry.
- **Garden boy (shamba boy)**: garden and grounds.
- **Watchman (askari)**: night guard, often armed with rungu or panga.

### Kitchen arrangement
- Typically a separate building from the main house, connected by covered walkway.
- Reasons: fire safety, heat management, colonial spatial separation.
- Within barbed-wire compound but physically separate.

### Family's household
- Cook: Charago (white hat, green apron; good with children but sometimes drunk).
- Kitchen boy: Katschutta (all practical household tasks).
- Aya: name possibly Wanjiko [uncertain].

## Women's roles

- **Memsahib** (Hindi-derived): directed all domestic operations.
- Planned menus, instructed cook, managed supplies, supervised cleaning.
- **Singer sewing machine**: ubiquitous. Made children's clothes, curtains, costumes.
- Drove long distances on unpaved roads. Rosemary drove a red DKW with white roof.
- Carried pepper spray in handbag. Handled snakes.
- Social obligations: club functions, horse races (formal dress), agricultural shows.
- Played football pools by post (British results arrived weeks late).

## Children's experience

### Schools
- Racially segregated: separate for Europeans, Asians, Africans.
- Started at 4-5 in kindergarten.
- Small schools, strict teaching: slapping, caning, rapping knuckles.
- During late colonial disruptions, schools relocated (e.g., kindergarten moved to club).

### Daily life
- Supervised by aya. Outdoor life on the farm.
- Many children spoke better Swahili than English in early years.
- Birthday treats: Nairobi National Park (est. 1946) or Snake Park.
- Swimming at Blue Post Hotel in Thika, by Chania Falls.
- Put in car in pyjamas when parents went to club parties; watchman checked on them.

## Language

- Swahili (Kiswahili) was lingua franca between settlers and African workers.
- Most settlers spoke "kitchen Swahili" — simplified, mixed with English.
- Children often spoke better Swahili than parents due to time with aya and staff.
- Kikuyu language also widely spoken in Central Province.
- English was official language of colony.

## Period details matching the family's memories

- **DKW cars**: German manufacturer (later Audi). Small, affordable two-stroke cars popular in East Africa in the 1950s. Likely DKW F89 model.
- **DDT spraying**: widely used for mosquito/malaria control. Normal practice until 1960s-70s. Rachel Carson's *Silent Spring* (1962) began turning opinion.
- **Mosquito nets**: standard in every bedroom. Malaria constant threat even at altitude.
- **Night train to Mombasa**: Uganda Railway/East African Railways, ~530 km, 12-16 hours. Standard way to reach coast.
