## Part 1: Before my childhood begins

### Opening

Tim: Hello, hello.

Mamma: Yes, it looks good.

Tim: Now I know it works. Have you thought about the order? We have to start with Jakob.

Tim: Let me just tell the AI that this is an interview with Elizabeth Prebensen, who is my mother. My name is Tim Knutsen. She was born in Kenya, and we are going to talk about her life in Kenya as a child, until she was around eight years old. It will be an unstructured, maybe messy interview, but it is only meant to be written down and worked on afterwards.

Tim: I have written down what you know about the central people and processes. Are you happy for us just to start there?

Mamma: Yes.

Tim: It is probably sensible to begin with what year you were born.

Mamma: Yes, yes. It is 1954.

Tim: Don't think about it. Just talk to me.

Mamma: Yes. I was born in 1954, to Jakob Prebensen from Risor and Rosemary Durant from England.

Tim: Why Kenya?

### Jakob and the road out of Norway

Mamma: I will try to say something about that now, for both of them. But then I want to begin with Jakob. He was born in 1916 in Risor. He would have been 110 years old today. He grew up in a fairly well-off family in Risor, back when shipping still meant status. They were shipowners.

Mamma: Later it did not go so well, but in any case his father moved to Gjerstad. I have to say this now, because it matters later. He bought a farm called Vik Gard, based on an inheritance his wife received from Denmark.

Tim: Where was he during the war?

Mamma: Before the war he had been on whaling in the Antarctic, on a ship out of Sandefjord.

Tim: That was your grandfather?

Mamma: No, I am talking about Jakob. Fritz Prebensen was my grandfather, the one who bought the farm. But in any case, Jakob had been whaling, then he came home, and then there was war. I do not know much about what he did during the war. He may have had some connection to the resistance, but I do not know much about it.

Tim: Is there anything written about that?

Mamma: No. I think there was a milieu in Gjerstad that he knew. But what I think happened was that, through contacts, another road opened up.

Mamma: One of those contacts was his cousin, Vesla Koren, who had moved out to Kenya with her husband, Dan Koren. That must have been around [year uncertain: circa 1946?], after the war had ended.

### Kenya as possibility

Mamma: To speak about Kenya, it was a British colony. The British were especially keen to get Europeans out there to buy land and start farms and cultivate. They had been doing that partly even before the war. The Koren family, for example, had been there before the war.

Mamma: Dan Koren went down because of that and took Jakob's cousin with him. I think it was through her that Jakob got the idea of going out there himself and trying a life in the settler world in Kenya.

Tim: So Koren was married to the cousin?

Mamma: Yes.

Tim: And where did they live when Jakob went out?

Mamma: Then I have to start talking about a book I found at the National Library called *Norwegians in Colonial Kenya*. Among other things it mentions a name that suddenly came back to me, and that my father talked a lot about: the Kopperud brothers. They were another Norwegian family who established themselves down there.

Tim: Do you trust the information in that book?

Mamma: Yes. Koren is mentioned a lot, and the cousin too. So I think that was part of why Jakob went out there, and also that he wanted to get away from Gjerstad. He had a need for that.

### Jakob's temperament and family wounds

Tim: Do you know much about why he did not want to stay there?

Mamma: There was a bad relationship between him and his parents. The Prebensen family is almost a chapter in itself. But there was a bad relationship between his father and mother and him.

Tim: What about the relationship between Fritz and his wife?

Mamma: I know nothing about that.

Tim: And do you know why he fled from it, in a way?

Mamma: According to him, they put big demands on him that he could not live up to. He was supposed to take over the farm. They wanted him to do well at school. He was already a very nervous type back then.

Tim: Did he say that himself?

Mamma: Yes. At the same time he had guts and a taste for adventure, since he had gone whaling. He had that. But he was also a very, very handsome man. I have understood that. He thought highly of himself and his looks. That too is something I remember.

Mamma: So in a way he was well equipped to come to Kenya and live in that world. He claimed he had been "engaged in his trousers" two or three times in Norway before he left.

Tim: "Engaged in his trousers"?

Mamma: Yes, yes. You understand the type. He was definitely colourful in his own way.

Tim: Did he have any safe adults in his life, or did he lack that?

Mamma: In Risor and Gjerstad? Yes. The servant girls. He said he had better relationships with them than with his own mother and father. So he was marked by it, and he dwelt on it far too much later in life. He would probably have needed modern psychotherapy to handle it properly.

### Jakob in Kenya

Mamma: I think coming out there was a huge experience for him. It must have been in 1947, I would guess, because they married in 1952, and he said he had been there six or seven years before he met her.

Mamma: In that period many people went out and settled in Kenya because it was exciting, there were opportunities, and there was little regulation. But you had to be at least somewhat comfortable financially. He had an agricultural education from Holt near Tvedestrand, so he had a background.

Tim: What did he do first?

Mamma: When he first arrived, he worked for Dan Koren for many years.

Tim: With coffee?

Mamma: No, then it was cattle. Dan Koren had cattle. Do you know where it was? In the highlands around Nairobi, in a place called Sianda [place spelling still to verify]. That was where he was.

Mamma: And then he got to read and hear all these stories about the good life down there, from the British point of view: lots of parties, lots of club life. They lived a life around the clubs.

Tim: And "club" is not a club in the modern sense.

Mamma: No, it is a meeting place. Explorers Club, colonial clubs, places like that. There is also a bit written about that in the book. So I think he must have loved it.

Tim: Are there any photos from that time?

Mamma: Not from that period itself. I have photos from after they married and I was born. But we do have films later.

### More about Jakob

Mamma: He had that adventurousness, with blue-whale whaling and all the rest of it, and of course the need to get away.

Mamma: But his nervousness is central. I think it was always there. He was a somewhat anxious man.

Tim: How old was he when he went out there?

Mamma: If he was born in 1916 and left when he did, then he was thirty or thirty-one. That age is probably worth including.

Mamma: What he left behind in Norway were two sisters. The older one, Minnie, had married Per Adler. He was in the resistance and was imprisoned at Grini. The younger sister left two years later, but for South Africa.

Tim: That is the branch of the family nobody really knows.

Mamma: Yes. We get occasional traces from that branch. She probably left for the same reason. But we have no real contact with them at all. So that too is a story of people wanting to get out.

### Rosemary

Tim: Then we can move over to Rose.

Mamma: Yes. Rosemary Durant. The famous one. The legend.

Tim: Is it alright if I speak English sometimes?

Mamma: Yes, yes.

Mamma: She was born in 1926 and would have turned one hundred on 23 March. She was very proud of being almost the same age as Queen Elizabeth.

Mamma: There is a strong story there. She was the daughter of a British career military man. He became a colonel later, not then, but he was career military and mostly active in India. He served in both world wars.

Mamma: He married Kathleen, who was half Scottish if not fully Scottish. What happened, and this was very unusual in those days, was that they divorced when Rosemary was four or five.

Tim: People just didn't do that.

Mamma: No, they didn't. I think the relatives in Australia dug into it and say adultery was listed in the papers. But that may just have been the official reason they had to use. In any case, they divorced.

Mamma: She really had no proper home to go back to. She went to school as a small child, I think in Wales, and that school may have been run by people who were Jehovah's Witnesses. She was in boarding school, or "cross school" as it came out in the transcript, from the time she was little until she was seventeen.

Mamma: The only real home feeling she had in the holidays was with her grandmother on her mother's side. She spoke warmly of her. But she was a child who did not have a proper home.

### The war and what she did not say

Tim: Then the war comes.

Mamma: Yes. She left school during the war and enlisted straight away. In the Wrens, the Women's Royal Naval Service, around 1944. She would have been seventeen or eighteen.

Tim: Is this where Enigma comes in?

Mamma: Yes. Many, many years later, and only after she died, we found out what she had actually done during the war. The relatives in Australia became very curious about it. They used me as the instrument, because I was her eldest and the only one who could get her records.

Mamma: She would never tell us directly what she really did. She always answered cryptically, as if she had only been one small link in a coding and decoding system. But after she died it came out that she had been part of the Enigma work. Her name is in Bletchley Park in England.

Tim: So perhaps she did not know the full scope herself?

Mamma: No, I don't think so. She knew what she was doing, but not necessarily how important it was in the big picture. She did a job she understood to be important. That is probably the right way to put it.

Mamma: After the war she was in her early twenties and worked, among other things, for the British Colour Council. That gave her a great feeling for colour. She was very good with colour, and very good at drawing.

### Kenya, and meeting Jakob

Mamma: Oliver Durant had by then married Cynthia, her stepmother. He was still in India. They had Christopher in India in 1945, and soon after that they moved to Kenya. So he was based there after the war.

Tim: So she had not seen her father in a very long time?

Mamma: No. It went a long time between meetings. But they wrote letters all the time. It is in her diaries. A lot of letters.

Mamma: So in 1952 Cynthia wrote and asked her to come out on holiday. That was how she came to Kenya.

Tim: And then she met Jakob?

Mamma: Yes. And here we have two versions. One is the more down-to-earth and probable one: Cynthia worked by taking milk samples around the farms, and Rosemary came with her into a cowshed where Jakob was standing with his arm far up the backside of a cow. That is one version.

Tim: And the other?

Mamma: The other, the one Jakob liked to tell, was that they met at a resort in Malindi on the coast, and that he more or less fell straight into her heart. That is nonsense as it stands, but he liked telling it.

Mamma: In any case it only took three or four months and they were married. It was one of those sudden infatuations that became a marriage. They married in 1952.

Tim: Why so fast? Was it just because that was what people did?

Mamma: It was probably both because it was more common to marry quickly once you had found the one, and because the whole situation was like that. Jakob used to call himself "her last chance in Africa." He said it jokingly, and it irritated her a bit.

Tim: But they had a good relationship?

Mamma: Yes, I think they did, despite him being how he was. I think they loved each other.

Mamma: And there is also this detail that says something about the difference between them: she was completely shocked that when she met him he could buy a new shirt every day instead of washing the old one. He could not be bothered washing it. So she took over immediately. They were a good match in that way.

Tim: And you are born in December 1954.

Mamma: Yes. And I cannot say much about the time from 1954 until the years I remember myself, except that in 1954 there was still full Mau Mau emergency or uprising in the background. That is probably a good place to pause.

Tim: Yes, we can pause there. These are the broad lines, and then we can dig deeper.

## Part 2: Childhood on the coffee farm

### Where we lived

Tim: Then we are at your life as a child in Kenya, as you remember it, freely told.

Mamma: Then I just have to take simple things, simple things I can talk around. Perhaps I should say where we lived. We lived in Thika, which is a small town outside Nairobi. It lies quite high up. Nairobi also lies high. And of course there is coffee.

Tim: Yes, and you have been there.

Mamma: Yes. That was where my father began to work and manage a coffee plantation in those years. It was called Rubislaw, and it belonged to people called Sutcliffe, who I think were British. Simply that.

### The farm and the little village

Mamma: The farm was remote. You remember that too, perhaps? But I remember it as remote. So the life they had there was very isolated in many ways.

Mamma: That was how it was on all the farms: the married couple were there with the children. It was just the two of them with children, and then you had people in the house. At Rubislaw there was also a small village. I remember it as something like the old Norwegian children's books: grass huts and mud huts.

Tim: Was it really like that?

Mamma: Yes. It was. They built them themselves on the estate. So they lived there, in what we called the village. Open fires, cooking, and they were the people who worked on the plantation and picked the coffee, especially the women.

Mamma: The machine transcription breaks down here, but the structure is clear enough: the married couple and children in the main house, people in the house, and a village on the estate for the workers. If we want every labour detail, it still needs checking against the audio.

### Coffee as memory

Mamma: Here we are getting into the coffee itself. When the berries had been picked, they were taken to what we called the coffee plant. All the red berries were poured into a large water centrifuge so the skin was separated from the two white beans.

Tim: The skin went out one way and the beans another?

Mamma: Yes. The skin came out one way, and the white beans came out into water channels another way. That was the process. Then the white beans were laid out on very long tables with a kind of sailcloth where they dried in the sun.

Mamma: That is also on one of the films, where the African women are standing and turning the beans with their arms. So we do actually have pictures of it.

Tim: Did Kenyans produce coffee before this, or is it a European system?

Mamma: I don't know. That is an interesting question. I do not think the machinery and the whole plant system were originally theirs. That was probably European. But that is something we need to find out. I only know how I remember it on that farm.

Mamma: The reason I remember it so well is that we sometimes walked from the main house down to the coffee plant. It was part of our childhood world. On the way we always looked for chameleons. If we found one in the roadside grass it was very exciting, because they moved so slowly and changed colour.

Tim: So you and Wenche could come back later and see if it had changed colour?

Mamma: Yes. Sometimes we moved it onto something else and then came back, and it had changed colour. We thought that was marvellous.

Mamma: And the other thing that made me remember the coffee beans so clearly was that sometimes we were allowed to help turn them. They lay in the sun and were warm. It was so nice and warm.

Tim: And Rosemary also used coffee from the farm at home?

Mamma: Yes. She took our own coffee, roasted it on the stove, ground it in a grinder, and made coffee from it. I remember that. She knew how to do it.

### Office, accounts, and the works

Mamma: That was where my father had his office as well. We went down to his office and visited him there. That was where he kept the accounts, and of course there were Africans operating the whole works. It was not he who physically did all of it.

Tim: So you saw both the work and the office side of it?

Mamma: Yes. It was all part of the same world.

### Wealth, hierarchy, and visits

Tim: Do you know more about Sutcliffe?

Mamma: I think Sutcliffe had several plantations, with different managers. They were certainly very well off. Sometimes we were taken to visit them, and then we were dressed up in our finest dresses, which she sewed for us on her little Singer machine. We were given strict instructions not to get dirty and to behave ourselves.

Tim: So they were, in a sense, the top colonial family in that setting?

Mamma: Yes, at least people with far more money and position.

### Snakes and fear

Mamma: Another thing connected to the coffee was that Jakob shot two pythons in the coffee field. The coffee bushes were quite high, so they could move around in there. Then he had people bring the snakes back. The skin was stretched on long boards and laid to dry on the billiard table, because they had a billiard room.

Tim: They played billiards in the evening?

Mamma: Yes. There was no television. So billiards and club life and guests were part of the evening world.

Mamma: Snakes were really a theme all the time. I especially remember a story from the house. It was a house on two levels, and you had to go down to get to the toilet, with the kitchen out on the courtyard beyond. He was sitting there with the door open for a long time, and then he discovered a large puff adder in the little room beyond.

Mamma: Then we only heard him roar, "Rosemary, get the children out." We had to get out of the house, the cook and the others too, and then he got hold of something and beat it to death.

Mamma: Then he carried it out and laid it on the cement outside the kitchen. The cat came over and looked at it. Then we heard hissing, and it turned out it was not quite dead after all. Then he had to go out and finish it properly.

Mamma: He also claimed he once found a mamba among our toys on the veranda. Whether it was exactly that species, I cannot say. But the point is that danger was a real part of daily life. That was why we were never alone.

### Aya, Swahili, kitchen

Mamma: We always had an aya, a nursemaid, watching us when we were outside. Her name may have been [possibly Wanjiko]. She was always nearby while we played.

Tim: Did she speak English?

Mamma: No, Swahili. We spoke more Swahili than anything else with her. Kitchen Swahili. The simplest words. We understood her, and many of the workers did not know much English.

Mamma: My father spoke Swahili with them all the time. Probably terrible Swahili, but he had to learn it. Rose could also speak Swahili.

Mamma: We had a cook called Charago. He had a white chef's hat and, I think, a green apron. He was said to be very good at cooking and good with children. We could talk a little with him. But according to the diaries he was also often drunk.

Mamma: Then they had a kitchen boy called Katschutta. He was a general handyman. He washed the car, polished floors, did all the practical work that kept the household going.

Mamma: The kitchen was always outside the main house, but inside the barbed-wire security area. It was a wood stove. There was no electricity. So all the food was made there under Rose's instructions.

Tim: But she also made things herself?

Mamma: Yes. In the evenings she made our supper on a little stove inside. That is actually an important detail, because not all settler women did that. Many left everything to the servants. But she did some things herself.

Tim: How did they treat the people working for them, really?

Mamma: That is the big question. She was not a military type, but of course she gave orders. It was a colonial system, whichever way you look at it. But she was not harsh in manner.

### Animals, house, and security

Mamma: We had chickens inside the compound. We ate them. We were allowed to watch when they cut their heads off. My father thought that was something we might as well see.

Mamma: We also had many rabbits, in hutches up off the ground so they would not be taken. We ate them too. She made rabbit stew. I ate a lot of rabbit as a child.

Mamma: We had two dogs, two Alsatians. Zipp, who was the family dog, and Elsa, who was the guard dog. Elsa was not one we were meant to get attached to in the same way. She was trained as a guard dog. We also had two cats, Shiba and Fanny.

Mamma: Rose had a beautiful garden, with many flowers, which she worked on. She was keen on gardening and had people helping with it. That too is in the diaries.

Tim: And Jakob was always armed?

Mamma: Yes. He was always in khaki and with a pistol. The whole lot. Always. That is also what connects back to the Mau Mau side discussion, that this state of readiness is one of my childhood memories.

### Nairobi, Christmas, and driving

Mamma: We spent a lot of time in Nairobi. We visited Oliver and Cynthia many times. They lived in an area called Spring Valley. We always looked forward to that.

Mamma: We celebrated Christmas Day there. Christmas Eve we had at Rubislaw, a Norwegian Christmas Eve, with presents and a tree, because Jakob wanted that. At Oliver and Cynthia's they also had a tree with real candles. It caught fire once. I remember that clearly.

Mamma: Another thing I remember from there is that when we were put to bed in the evening, we were laid in Oliver and Cynthia's bed. Then their cat, Bambo, came and lay by my feet. Chris was very impressed afterwards that I still remembered the name Bambo.

Mamma: My mother drove us around in a DKW, red with a white roof. She did not drive slowly. She drove fast.

### School and Miss Foiret

Tim: When did you start school?

Mamma: When I was four. First kindergarten. Then I began at an ordinary primary school, only for white or European children. I liked that better.

Mamma: But the first teacher, especially in kindergarten, was strict. She was called Miss Foiret. She was not kind. She slapped us, rapped our fingers, and ruled the place.

Tim: So you learned a lot, but through fear?

Mamma: Yes. It was drilled into us. I could add, multiply, divide, and read early. But it was not because it was cosy.

Mamma: When things started changing politically, and the school was being closed or opened to Africans, I was moved back to kindergarten for a while, when the school had been moved up to the club. That teacher later wrote letters to Rose and almost wanted to come to Norway. Then Wenche and I protested. And Jakob said no. We are not having that here.

### Club life, children in the car, and later memories

Mamma: She drove us back and forth without seat belts, very fast, on dusty roads. She had pepper spray in her handbag in case the car broke down and someone tried something. That too was part of daily life.

Mamma: The club in Thika was the gathering place for settlers. Norwegians, Danes, Britons, and other Europeans met there. If they were going to a party there, we were taken along in the car in our pyjamas and laid in the back seat while they were inside at the party. A watchman came from time to time and looked in to check everything was alright.

Tim: Were you frightened?

Mamma: No. That was just how it was. But today it sounds completely mad. And I am quite sure she drove home in pitch darkness while he was not sober.

### Language, birthdays, and outings

Mamma: We visited Dan and Vesla Koren several times. They had two children. I remember getting out of the car, I must have been four or five, and then suddenly my father started speaking Norwegian. I understood nothing. That was the first time it dawned on me that he came from another country.

Mamma: Their children were bilingual, because both parents were Norwegian. I think if it had been the other way round, if Rose had been Norwegian, then we would have learned Norwegian much earlier.

Mamma: On our birthdays we could choose between Nairobi National Park and the snake park in Nairobi. That was "the birthday treat".

Mamma: In the national park we drove around and saw animals from the car. Wildebeest, zebra, whole herds crossing the road. The most frightening thing was when baboons came and sat on the roof of the car while we were standing still. Then all the windows had to go up, and they bent down and peered in.

Mamma: We had picnics. Once a monkey jumped in and stole food. It took one thing in its mouth and more in its hands and disappeared again. It was both frightening and utterly fascinating.

Mamma: We also celebrated birthdays at the club, with lots of cakes and jelly. I have never liked jelly since, because I was forced to eat it.

### Sports Day, Blue Post, theatre, Malindi

Mamma: At school we had Sports Day. Very British. Formations, flags, the whole thing. I think that is on film as well.

Mamma: When Rose went shopping in Thika, she had her own shops and tailors, often run by Indians. We bought English chocolate. She did "the big shop". That is in the diaries too.

Mamma: We went to the Blue Post Hotel in Thika to swim. That was where I learned to swim. There were separate times for Europeans and others. We were fascinated by Sikhs who swam and took off their turbans.

Mamma: Rose made little theatre performances with us. She sewed costumes, hand-painted programmes, and worked away in the evenings on her Singer machine. She could be quite cross if she was disturbed when she was making something, but that was also an important creative side of her.

Mamma: She also played the football pools, or some sort of betting. "Did not win much," it says in the diaries.

Mamma: And then we went to the horse races. That was very exciting. She dressed up in fine 1950s dresses and a hat. We were dressed up too. We could stand and look at the horses and point to the one we thought was the prettiest. If it won and she had not backed it, she got annoyed.

Mamma: We also had picnics by the car with hard-boiled eggs. I remember that well.

Mamma: We went on holiday to Malindi a couple of times. We flew down to the coast in a propeller plane and were sick as dogs because it was so bumpy.

Mamma: There we stayed in a bungalow on the beach. I remember the pink crabs running along. We swam in the sea and in a big pool. It was sun, swimming, getting burnt, being rubbed with lotion. It was exciting.

Mamma: On the way into Rubislaw we drove through huge sisal fields and over mounds or termite hills that made the car lurch violently. We thought that was fun. She drove fast, and Jakob was furious.

Mamma: We always slept under mosquito nets. Before we went to bed, they sprayed the room with DDT. Today it sounds unbelievable, but it was part of life.

## Supplemental recording: Mau Mau side conversation

Editorial note: this section comes from a separate short recording, not from the same continuous interview file as Part 2.

Tim: I have a little side track about Mau Mau. Can we take it briefly?

Mamma: Yes, but I am not a historian, just so that is clear.

Tim: That is fine.

Mamma: But I have tried to think about it a bit and read a little. Because I only got one side of it from Jakob.

Tim: Yes.

Mamma: The reality was that Kenya was a British colony. They encouraged people to come out there and cultivate land they had taken from Africans. They did not buy it; they took it.

Tim: With the military?

Mamma: I do not know. With an argument that they knew how to cultivate land better. But this is something we need to dig more deeply into.

Mamma: They probably also set groups against each other and supported one side against another. I think there is something about that in the reading.

Tim: So how have you understood Mau Mau itself?

Mamma: As I understand it, Mau Mau emerged as a violent movement, especially with roots in Kikuyu environments. But here it is important that I do not pretend I know this fully. Some of this I have read later, and some of it is how it was talked about in the family.

Mamma: What people at the time called a "terror organisation" was also part of a colonial way of speaking. At the same time, I remember it being told as something dangerous and brutal. And the Kikuyu were split as well: some were loyal to the settlers and worked on the farms, and some supported the uprising.

Mamma: I have also read that people could be pressured, influenced, and drawn into it. So I try to keep it open, because I know it was not as simple as it was presented to us.

Tim: And Jakob's role?

Mamma: The authorities responded hard. It was not only military measures, but also civilian settler preparedness. Jakob was part of that world.

Mamma: That was why he was always armed. And that really is one of my own childhood memories: that he always carried a weapon.

Mamma: And then you can think about how those farms were built. There was barbed wire around the main house, fairly high. A gate. A watchman. Dogs at night. I remember that.

Tim: But you do not have direct memories from the uprising itself?

Mamma: No. I was born in 1954, and the uprising was already going on. That means I do not have my own memories from the first and most intense phase. It is important to distinguish between what I actually remember and what I was told later or read later.

Tim: So much of this is something you got later?

Mamma: Yes. Either told in the family or read afterwards. And then there is the ugly side of it, that the British put many of those they captured into camps and treated them terribly. That is something I read later, and which was never talked about back then.

Tim: So it is important to distinguish between the family's version and what you later read?

Mamma: Yes. Exactly. Because I have no direct memories of the whole Mau Mau business itself. That is before I could reflect on it. But I remember the atmosphere left behind, and the security systems around the farms.

Tim: When did Kenya become independent?

Mamma: Not until 1963.

Tim: So it makes sense to fill in some historical things around your memories.

Mamma: Yes, I think that is sensible. There is a lot on the internet alone. But we have to be careful to distinguish between what I actually remember, what I got from Jakob, and what comes from later reading.

## Part 3: Leaving Kenya

### The end begins

Mamma: Now we come to the ending, you could say, of life in Kenya. We are in 1962, when it began to be clear to many Europeans down there that the era was coming to an end.

Mamma: It was gradually decided that Kenya would become independent. I noticed that too, as I probably said, because the school I was going to was closed. In the last period I went back to the kindergarten I had previously attended.

Mamma: By then my parents had begun to talk about having to leave the country, and where they should go. It has been said that he considered Rhodesia for a while, what is now Zimbabwe, because the white regime held out much longer there. But he gave up that idea.

Tim: And South Africa?

Mamma: South Africa was not an option. He was not for apartheid.

Mamma: So the decision became to go back to Norway, to Gjerstad and Vik. He wrote to his parents and to his sister Minnie saying they wanted to come back.

### The letter and the breakdown

Mamma: I can remember waking in the middle of the night at Rubislaw. I heard him crying, got up, and ran into the sitting room, and there he was in a chair with a letter in his hand, weeping.

Tim: Wow. Have you understood afterwards what it was?

Mamma: Yes. Afterwards I understood that it was a reply from his parents, where they quite simply did not think it was such a good idea that he should come home. He was not especially welcome.

Mamma: That was when he got what was called "the so-called nervous breakdown." I was sent back to bed. I do not know if Wenche remembers this, but I do.

### Packing and farewell

Mamma: After that a return journey was prepared. She packed large crates with various things. Among them the little chair over there that we still have.

Mamma: This was the autumn, toward Christmas, 1962. We were going to take a ship from Mombasa down to Cape Town and then on from there. The idea was that this would also be some kind of recuperation for Jakob, that he would recover. He did not.

Mamma: For us children it was exciting. We stopped kindergarten, and we knew we were going to Norway. But the only time I have seen my mother truly upset, with tears streaming down her face, was when she was leaving that life behind.

### The long journey out

Mamma: We took the train from Nairobi down to Mombasa. It took all night. That was exciting. Then we went aboard the ship. The shipping line was The Castle Line. The first ship we were on was called Kenya Castle [ship name still to verify].

Mamma: We stopped at several places down the east coast. Zanzibar, for instance. But because he was so unwell, we could not go ashore on the organised excursions. We thought that was disappointing.

Mamma: We were in Durban and Port Elizabeth in South Africa. There we met Alexandra [surname unclear], a relative on Wenche's side, and her husband. There was also a cousin called Alexandra Wenche, around twelve years old, I think. I was eight. We were in a park and ate with them.

Mamma: Then we went on to Cape Town, where we changed ships. We went ashore there. I can still see Table Mountain in my mind. We were in a park feeding birds and squirrels.

Mamma: Then we boarded the next ship, Pretoria Castle. Whether that journey took two or three weeks I do not know, but it was a long sea voyage.

Mamma: We were not in first class, we were in second class. But we were allowed to swim in the ship's pool. That was fun because the ship made waves in the pool when the sea was rough. Wenche could not swim and had a rubber ring.

Mamma: I also remember that one time we were invited to eat at the Captain's Table. Rose thought that was very grand. There were also children's games arranged on board.

Mamma: But the whole journey was marked by the fact that he stayed in the cabin a great deal and was depressed. Afterwards I understood that the ship's doctors were so concerned that they set one of the staff to keep watch on him because they thought he might be suicidal.

### England and Norway

Mamma: We arrived in Southampton and were met by our Scottish grandmother and Reggie. It very quickly became clear that he had to see a doctor, that he was very ill.

Mamma: He made contact with Minnie and Per Adler in Oslo, and it was decided that he should travel over in advance for treatment. Per Adler had nerve problems himself and had also been admitted to Vinderen, so he had contacts.

Mamma: Jakob was sent by plane and admitted. We stayed behind in England with grandmother and Reggie, in some sort of boarding house. Then we were also with [name unclear: Orsela / Ursula?], who was a cousin of Rose, in a little village called Great Chisel [place spelling still to verify] outside Cambridge.

Mamma: We lived in a little cottage there for a few weeks. At the same time he was treated, including with shock treatment.

Mamma: After that we were to go on to Norway and stay with Dan and Vesla Koren at Jevnaker. They had moved home several months before us and bought a farm by Randsfjorden called Stangard.

Mamma: That was our first meeting with Norway on land. First we had been in England, where it was just rain. Then we came to the farm country at Jevnaker. I liked it there, with all the animals. It was the first time I picked ceps.

Mamma: Eventually he recovered enough that we could move to Gjerstad. It was late autumn. The transition was a great shock for Rose. The climate was completely different. Suddenly we had to wear masses of clothes, tights, trousers, everything. It was the first time we really felt what cold was.

Mamma: And then it turned out that the farm in Gjerstad was completely run down. That was a great disappointment and a shock for Jakob.

### Later reflections

Mamma: After the winter of 1963-64 it was decided that we would live in Risor. I started in fourth grade and Wenche in second. In Gjerstad I had been in third, but the headmaster thought that since I could already add, multiply, divide and so on, I had to go into a higher class.

Mamma: There was a teacher there who knew English. That helped, because that was when we began to learn Norwegian properly. At first we spoke what people called Gjerstad-English.

Mamma: I remember reading aloud in class and the whole class laughing because it sounded so odd. But the teacher was good and said they could laugh now, but when they themselves started English in two years, they would not laugh so much.

Mamma: We learned quickly. We could stand in the doorway and speak English in toward Rose and Norwegian out. At the same time Wenche and I began to get our own circles of friends. We had always been together, so that was new.

Mamma: Fritz Prebensen died in 1965. Then the farm was sold. I do not think there was much to be got from it, to be honest. He was not especially good with money.

Mamma: Jakob started at the pulp factory in Risor. They needed people, so he quickly got sea work there. But it was physically demanding work, and he was not physically strong. The transition from seventeen years in the tropics to the climate in Risor was a great strain.

Mamma: After some years he was put on disability. He also had several further breakdowns later. When Gry was born in 1976 he had a serious collapse and spent a year at Kokkplassen in Arendal. That was when they used electroshock again, and then lithium came. I actually think lithium kept him stable for the rest of his life.

Tim: It sounds like bipolarity.

Mamma: Yes, that may well be. He was very up and down. Wenche partly agrees with that as well.

Mamma: But that is really the story afterwards. The important thing here is the transition: how a whole life in Kenya came to an end, how the departure was dramatic, and how everything had to be rebuilt in a completely different climate and country.
