## Draft

### Before I was born

I was born in Kenya in 1954, but to explain my childhood properly I have to begin with my parents, and especially with my father, Jacob Prebensen from Risor in southern Norway.

He was born in 1916 into a family that had once had money and standing in the old shipping world. Later things went badly. The family moved to Gjerstad, where my grandfather Fritz bought a farm called Vik Gard with money from an inheritance his wife had received from Denmark. Gjerstad runs like a fault line through the whole story. It was where my father came from, and also the place he kept trying to get away from.

Before the war he had been on whaling expeditions in the Antarctic, so there was adventure in him, and courage too. But he was also already nervous by temperament. That was not just something that appeared later. It was there from early on. He said himself that his parents demanded things of him he could not become: the son who would take over the farm, do well at school, and carry the family forward. If he ever spoke warmly about anyone from that childhood, it was more likely to be the servant girls than his own parents.

He was a divided man in that way. Anxious, but also vain, handsome, and highly conscious of himself. He thought a great deal of his own looks and could be very colourful. He claimed he had been "engaged in his trousers" a couple of times before he ever left Norway. It is a ridiculous phrase, but it tells you something true about him. Colonial Kenya, with its clubs, rituals, social theatre, and possibilities for reinvention, must have suited that side of him very well.

After the war an opening appeared. His cousin Vesla Koren had gone out to Kenya with her husband, Dan Koren. Kenya was then a British colony, and the whole settler system was built on Europeans being encouraged to go out, take land, and make lives there. Through Vesla and Dan, my father got the idea that he too could go. I think it was both attraction and escape. Kenya drew him, and Gjerstad pushed him away.

He left around 1947, in his early thirties. At first he worked for Dan Koren, who kept cattle in the highlands around Nairobi, in a place that appears in the interview as Cicenda [spelling still uncertain]. He had agricultural training from Holt near Tvedestrand, so he had a practical background. But it was not only practical opportunity that mattered. He stepped into a colonial world of club life, parties, and a sense of freedom. For a while, I think he must have loved it.

My mother, Rosemary Durant, came from an entirely different background. She was born in 1926 into a British military family. Her father was a career officer, much of the time in India. Her mother, Kathleen, was Scottish, wholly or partly, and the extraordinary thing for that period was that her parents divorced when Rosemary was still very small. One simply did not do that then. The result was that she grew up as a child with no proper home to return to.

She was sent to school in Wales while still very young and spent almost all her upbringing in boarding schools. In the holidays she stayed with her grandmother on her mother's side, whom she spoke of warmly, but the basic fact of her life was that she had to manage on her own. That became part of her character. She could be warm, but she was also disciplined, practical, and very used to standing alone.

During the war she joined the Wrens. She almost never spoke plainly about what she had done. Only after her death did we discover that she had been part of the Enigma work and that her name now stands at Bletchley Park. Afterwards she worked, among other things, for the British Colour Council. She had a gift for colour, she could draw, and she had a strong visual sense. That stayed with her all her life.

After the war her father remarried, to Cynthia. They had a son, Christopher, in India in 1945, and shortly afterwards moved to Kenya. There they had another son, Anthony, in 1947. Anthony was Rosemary's half-brother. In 1952 Cynthia wrote to Rosemary and invited her out on holiday. That was how she came there.

And then she met my father.

There were always two versions of that story. The earthy one, and to my mind the more likely one, was that Cynthia was driving around farms taking milk samples and Rosemary came into a cowshed with her just as Jacob was standing there with his arm deep inside a cow. The other was my father's preferred version, that they met at a resort in Malindi and he plunged straight into her heart. That version is almost certainly embellished, but he liked telling it. He wanted the story told with romance, not manure.

In any case they married after only three or four months. He jokingly called himself "her last chance in Africa," which irritated her. But I do think they had a good marriage, despite his difficulties. She very quickly took charge of the practical side of life. She was appalled to discover that before she arrived he might buy a new shirt rather than wash the old one. In that respect, at least, they were well matched.

### Rubislaw

By the time I was born, in December 1954, and later my sister Wenche in January 1957, the Mau Mau emergency still formed part of the background in Kenya. I do not have direct memories of the uprising itself. That is important. What I do remember is what remained of it in the atmosphere: the guns, the barbed wire, the watchmen, the dogs, the whole security apparatus around the farms. That was the world I entered.

My childhood landscape was Rubislaw, near Thika, outside Nairobi, in a high coffee district.

The place felt isolated. The farm was remote, like a small world cut off from everything else. The family lived in the main house, and around it were the workers, the kitchen, the office, the routine of the estate, and the little village where the labourers lived in grass and mud houses that later reminded me of old illustrations in Norwegian children's books. There were open fires, outdoor cooking, and women working with the coffee crop.

As a child I did not analyse any of this. It was simply how life was. But looking back, it is impossible not to see that everything was organised through a colonial system. The house, the village, the kitchen, the accounting office, the workers, the security, all of it belonged to a single order we lived inside without questioning.

Coffee is one of my deepest memories. When the red berries had been picked, they were taken to the coffee plant, where the outer skin was separated from the two white beans. The beans were spread on long tables to dry in the sun. I remember the warmth of them in my hands. I remember being allowed to help turn them. I remember walking down from the house and looking for chameleons on the way. Wenche and I would sometimes move one onto a different surface and come back later to see whether it had changed colour.

My mother also used the coffee at home. She roasted it, ground it, and made it herself. That matters, because she was not the sort of settler woman who simply handed everything over. Yes, she had people working for her, but she also did things herself. The kitchen was outside the main house, inside the barbed-wire security perimeter, and meals were made there under her instructions. But in the evening she made our supper on a little stove inside. That was her. She was not harsh in manner, but she gave orders. It was a colonial system whichever way one turned it.

My father had his office down by the coffee plant, where he kept the accounts while others ran the machinery and the work itself. We visited him there. That matters to my memory too. Work and home were not separate spheres. Everything belonged to the same physical world.

### Daily life

Some of the strongest memories are not dramatic at all. They are material. The sisal fields on the way into the farm. The termite mounds that made the car lurch. The mosquito nets over our beds. The rooms being sprayed with DDT in the evening. The warm coffee beans. The smell of dust and heat.

We had an aya watching us when we were outside, and we spoke a great deal of Swahili with her and with the others around us. My father's Swahili was probably dreadful, but he used it all the time. My mother could speak it too. We lived inside a multilingual everyday life long before I understood that this was anything unusual.

I remember the cook, Charago, in his white hat and green apron, and the kitchen boy, Katschutta, who did all the practical jobs that kept the household moving. I remember that the diaries said Charago could be both good with children and often drunk. These diaries were my mother's: she kept one on a daily basis her whole life. That, too, belongs to the truth of the place. It was not pure idyll, and it was not one simple moral story either.

We had chickens and rabbits and ate them. My father thought we might as well watch the chickens being beheaded. We had two Alsatians, Zipp and Elsa, and Elsa was the guard dog, not one we were meant to love in the same way. We had cats. We had a garden. My mother loved flowers and gardening, and the garden was beautiful. That contrast says something essential about the colonial household: on one side fear, security, and readiness; on the other side beauty, order, and performance.

My father always wore khaki and carried a handgun. The full outfit. That was part of the atmosphere all the time. So were the snake stories. The puff adder in the house. The cry of "Rosemary, get the children out." The snake laid out on the cement and found not to be quite dead. The mamba said to have been found among our toys on the veranda. Whether every detail was exactly as later told is not the point. The sense of danger was real.

### The settler world

There was also the other world: the clubs, the parties, the visits, the polished side of settler life. The Sutcliffe family, who owned the plantation, had more money and more standing than we did. When we visited them we were dressed in the finest dresses my mother made on her Singer machine and told not to get dirty.

The club in Thika was the social centre for Norwegians, Danes, Britons, and others. If our parents went to a party there, Wenche and I might be put in the back seat of the car in our pyjamas while a watchman came from time to time to look in on us. I was not afraid. That was simply normal then, though it sounds mad now.

We spent a lot of time in Nairobi with Oliver and Cynthia, who lived in Spring Valley, a grand residential area with large houses. We celebrated Christmas Day there, while Christmas Eve was kept in the Norwegian way back on the farm because that mattered to my father. One year their Christmas tree, lit with real candles, caught fire. I remember their cat, Bumble, curling up by my feet when we were put to bed there.

My mother drove everywhere in a red DKW with a white roof, and she drove very fast. She was not a slow, cautious woman. She carried pepper spray in her handbag in case the car broke down, but she still drove along dusty roads at speed, without seat belts, often with us in the car. I am quite sure she also drove home from the club in darkness while my father was not entirely sober. That image says more than any tidy social description could.

School belonged to this same world. I started at four, first in kindergarten and then at a school for white or European children. I liked the school better, but the first teacher, Miss Foiret, was strict and brutal. She slapped us and rapped our fingers. We learned, yes, but much of it was forced in through fear. Later, when politics began to shift and schools were being closed or opened to Africans, I was moved back for a time to the kindergarten, which had itself been moved to the club. School, club, and settler life all bled into one another.

One of my strangest memories is the moment I realised my father truly belonged somewhere else. We were visiting Dan and Vesla Koren when I heard him suddenly start speaking Norwegian. I understood nothing. It was as if a hidden door had opened. Their children were bilingual because both parents were Norwegian. We were not. In our home, English was the first language, and Norway was still somewhere else.

Birthdays could mean Nairobi National Park or the snake park, "the birthday treat." We saw zebra, wildebeest, and baboons on the roof of the car. We had picnics. Once a monkey sprang in and stole food. At the club there were cakes and jelly, and I have never liked jelly since, because I was made to eat it.

We went to the Blue Post Hotel in Thika to swim. That was where I learned to swim. We watched Sikhs bathing and removing their turbans. My mother did "the big shop" in town. At school we had Sports Day, all flags and formations, very British. At the kindergarten school she made little theatre productions with us, sewing costumes and hand-painting the programmes, though she could be quite sharp if she was interrupted. She played the football pools, went to horse races in 1950s dresses and hats, and could be put out if the prettiest horse won when she had not backed it.

We also went to Malindi on the coast. I remember the propeller flight being so rough that we were sick as dogs. I remember the bungalow on the beach, the pink crabs running sideways, the swimming, the heat, the sense of holiday. Childhood was not only system and politics. It was also sensation: light, animals, nausea, laughter, sand, and sun.

### Leaving Kenya

Then 1962 came, and it became clear that the era was ending. Kenya was moving toward independence, and many Europeans understood they would have to leave. My parents began to talk about where we might go. My father apparently considered Rhodesia for a while, but gave up the idea. South Africa was not an option for him because he was not in favour of apartheid. So the plan became Norway, Gjerstad, and Vik.

The emotional turning point, in my memory, is one night at Rubislaw when I woke and heard him crying. I ran into the sitting room and found him there with a letter in his hand. Later I understood that it was a reply from his parents making it clear he was not especially welcome home. That was when he had what people called a breakdown. The memory binds the whole story together: the need to flee Norway, the attempt to build a new life in Kenya, and the pain of being forced back toward the place he had once left.

After that the packing began. Large wooden crates were filled. We were to travel from Mombasa by ship, via Cape Town and onward. For us children it was exciting. We were going to Norway. But the only time I have ever seen my mother truly devastated, with tears streaming down her face, was when she was saying goodbye to her life in Kenya. She had arrived there as a young woman with no fixed home and was now leaving it as a grown woman with children, routines, and an entire life built around that place.

We took the night train to Mombasa and boarded the Kenya Castle. Later we changed to the Pretoria Castle. We stopped at Zanzibar, Durban, Port Elizabeth, and Cape Town. I remember Table Mountain. I remember the swimming pool on the ship, with the sea making waves in the water. I remember Wenche's rubber ring. I remember how proud my mother was to eat at the Captain's Table. But the whole journey was overshadowed by one fact: my father was very ill.

In England he was sent on for treatment while we stayed behind with relatives before travelling on to Norway and Jevnaker, where Vesla and Dan Koren were then living. After that came Gjerstad, the shock of cold, and then Risor and language and a completely different life. But that is already another chapter.

What remains when I look back on my childhood in Kenya is not only the historical outline, though it is always there. It is also the texture of it: coffee beans in the sun, khaki and the handgun, the chameleons by the roadside, Miss Foiret, Bumble the cat, the pink crabs in Malindi, the fast car, the open village, the garden, the language, fear and freedom all mixed together.

It was a childhood inside a colonial system, and it cannot be told honestly without saying that plainly. But it was also my childhood. It was full of sights, smells, people, strange details, love, unease, laughter, and things whose full meaning I only understood much later.

It is to be said that Jacob recovered gradually, and the couple had a full and pleasant life in Risor, where they both died.
